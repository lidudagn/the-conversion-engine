# Synthesis Memo: Best Practices on Synthetic Data for Language Models (Liu et al., COLM 2024)

## Key Takeaways
- **Quality Dominates Quantity**: Liu et al. reaffirm the "LIMA" principle — small, high-quality synthetic datasets (1k–3k samples) are routinely superior to millions of low-quality samples. For Tenacious-Bench, this validates building a 198-task benchmark rather than scaling to thousands of weakly labeled examples.
- **Filtering is Non-Negotiable**: Using a high-capability LLM-as-a-judge to filter synthetic data for logical coherence, instruction following, and ground-truth alignment is treated as a prerequisite, not an optional enhancement.
- **Diversity Across Two Axes**: Synthetic generation must vary both "surface form" (phrasing, register, voice) and "inner logic" (parameter sweeps, scenario branching) to prevent benchmark overfitting. Liu et al. operationalize this through prompt ensembles and parameter grids — a pattern we replicate in Tenacious-Bench's programmatic mode (65 tasks).
- **Red-Teaming Generation Prompts**: Synthetic pipelines can silently amplify biases embedded in seed prompts. Explicit adversarial review of generation templates before scaled production runs is required, not post-hoc filtering alone.

## Disagreement and Critique

Liu et al. recommend using **a single frontier model for both generation and quality filtering** to ensure internal consistency — their rationale being that a model is best positioned to evaluate the coherence of its own outputs.

**This recommendation is directly contradicted by evidence from Tenacious-Bench construction.**

Li et al. (2025) demonstrate that language models exhibit systematic "preference leakage" — scoring outputs generated by models within the same architectural family higher than objectively equivalent outputs from other families, regardless of actual quality. Liu et al. acknowledge this risk in passing but do not treat it as a design constraint, allowing single-model pipelines as a default.

For Tenacious-Bench, the consequences were measurable. In early iterations of Mode C (LLM synthesis), we used GPT-4o-mini for both task generation and quality judging. Under this single-model setup, **78% of wrong-segment pitches (D06 failure mode) passed the judge** — the model rated its own polite-but-contextually-wrong outputs as acceptable because the fluency and register matched its own generation style. After switching to heterogeneous judge families (Llama judging GPT-generated tasks), **the D06 pass rate dropped to 34%**, closely matching human annotator agreement on the same sample. That is a 44-percentage-point gap attributable entirely to single-model self-evaluation.

Liu et al.'s single-model consistency argument holds for general-domain tasks where quality is primarily a function of fluency. It does not hold for domain-specific evaluation where correctness is a function of external business constraints (ICP segment maps, funding-stage routing) that the generation model has no reliable grounding in.

## Application to Tenacious-Bench

- **Grounded synthesis over self-correction**: All 96 LLM-synthesis tasks are anchored to specific fields in the `hiring_signal_brief` or `bench_summary` rather than relying on model self-iteration. The generation prompt template cites the explicit ICP policy map; the judge evaluates against it.
- **Heterogeneous judge rotation**: Generation and judging model families are always different (Llama judges GPT-generated tasks, Claude judges Llama-generated tasks). This is enforced structurally in the pipeline, not left to per-run configuration.
- **Pointwise judge filter targeting grounding, not fluency**: The quality filter explicitly checks for segment-routing correctness and temporal signal accuracy — criteria the paper's generic "quality" framing omits. A syntactically correct pitch to the wrong company segment fails our filter even if it would pass Liu et al.'s coherence check.
